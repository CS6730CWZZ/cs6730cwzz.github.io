A Data-Driven Narrative

The Shifting Weight
of Death

How the world changed the way it dies, 1990–2019. A story told through 204 countries, 31 causes, and three decades of data.

Causes of Death - Our World In Data · 1990–2019 · 204 Countries

In 1990, the world recorded approximately 43.5 million deaths from the 31 tracked causes in our dataset. By 2019, that number had climbed to 54.4 million — a 24.9% increase, driven largely by population growth and aging. But the raw total hides the real story: the composition of death changed dramatically.

In 1990, the world lost millions to what are sometimes called the diseases of poverty — diarrhea, respiratory infections, complications at birth. These were the killers of children, of rural communities, of people the global economy had left behind. By 2019, something fundamental had shifted. The old enemies were in retreat, replaced by slower, quieter adversaries: heart disease, cancer, diabetes.

This isn't just a medical story. It is a story about toilets and highways, vaccines and cigarettes, prosperity and its price. Over three decades, as nations developed, their mortality profiles transformed — a process epidemiologists call the epidemiological transition.

The numbers are drawn from a dataset covering 204 countries and territories over 30 years (1990–2019), tracking 31 causes of death. The visualizations are built in Tableau and woven into the narrative — because data, without context, is just noise.

Chapter One

The Killers, Ranked

What kills the most people? The answer is immediately clear — and the shift over three decades reveals both triumph and trade-off.

Figure 1 places the top 10 global causes of death side by side for 1990 and 2019. Cardiovascular diseases dominate both years, climbing from 12,062,179 deaths in 1990 to 18,552,218 in 2019 — a growth of +53.8%. Neoplasms (cancers) rose even faster: from 5,752,596 to 10,074,275, surging by +75.1%. Together in 2019, these two categories accounted for 28.6 million of the world's 54.4 million tracked deaths — over half.

Figure 1 · Global Top 10 Causes of Death: 1990 vs 2019 — Horizontal bar chart comparing death counts across three decades.

But the chart also tells a second, quieter story of triumph. Diarrheal diseases fell from 2,894,957 to 1,533,777 deaths (−47.0%). Neonatal disorders declined from 3,004,581 to 1,881,372 (−37.4%). Lower respiratory infections dropped from 3,318,264 to 2,491,472 (−24.9%). Tuberculosis fell from 1,776,727 to 1,179,234 (−33.6%).

These are not small numbers. They represent millions of children who survived to see their fifth birthday because a village got a clean water source, a clinic stocked oral rehydration salts, or a mother received prenatal care.

One cause moved against the trend of declining infections: HIV/AIDS surged from 336,059 deaths in 1990 to 863,056 in 2019 (+156.8%), a reminder that new pandemics can emerge even as old ones retreat.

One Planet, Two Realities

Global averages mask an enormous divide. In 2019, where you lived still determined how you were likely to die.

In the upper-left corner of the scatter plot, African nations cluster together. Nigeria attributes just 11.4% of its deaths to cardiovascular diseases, while infectious conditions account for a full 50.0%. In the lower-right, Russia reaches a staggering 56.5% cardiovascular mortality, with infectious diseases at only 3.4%.

India (CVD 29.2%, Infectious 18.5%) straddles the middle — still losing people to diarrhea (632,345 deaths) and tuberculosis (422,634) while cardiovascular disease has already become the single largest killer at 2,574,410 deaths. India is living the transition in real time.

China (CVD 43.9%, Infectious 2.6%) has largely completed the shift. With 4,584,273 cardiovascular deaths and 2,716,551 cancer deaths, chronic diseases now account for the vast majority of its 10.4 million annual deaths — the highest national total in the world.

A Map Written in Heart Disease

Eastern Europe and Central Asia are the deepest red. Russia leads at 56.5% — meaning more than one in every two deaths is cardiovascular. This is a legacy of post-Soviet health system collapse, high alcohol and tobacco consumption, and limited preventive care.

Sub-Saharan Africa appears pale on the map — Nigeria at 11.4%, for instance. But this is not good news. People in these countries die of other things before heart disease gets a chance. When malaria (191,106 deaths in Nigeria alone) and diarrhea (181,138) kill so many young, fewer survive long enough to develop atherosclerosis.

Western Europe and Japan sit in a different category — wealthy but moderate. Japan's 27.2% and Germany's 39.1% reflect strong prevention programs, healthy diets, and universal healthcare.

The irony is inescapable: low cardiovascular mortality in Africa does not mean good health. Development saves lives from infection but opens the door to chronic disease. There is no free pass.

Six Nations, Six Portraits

Japan is the only major nation where cancer (32.3%, 442,474 deaths) surpasses heart disease (27.2%, 372,483 deaths) as the leading killer — reflecting exceptional cardiovascular prevention alongside one of the world's oldest populations. Notably, Alzheimer's accounts for 12.0% in Japan, far higher than most nations.

Nigeria's top killers are neonatal disorders (13.6%), malaria (13.3%), diarrheal diseases (12.6%), and lower respiratory infections (12.0%) — all conditions preventable with basic interventions. Cardiovascular diseases rank fifth at just 11.4%.

And then there is South Africa, the outlier among outliers. HIV/AIDS accounts for 28.5% of all deaths (143,851 out of 505,389) — the single largest cause in any of the six nations.

Two Countries, Two Worlds

In the United States (2,834,964 total deaths), the treemap is concentrated. Cardiovascular diseases (33.8%) and neoplasms (27.3%) together account for 61.1% of all deaths. Death in America is old, predictable, and chronic. Infectious diseases are almost invisible at 3.6%.

Nigeria's treemap (1,439,500 total deaths) is dispersed. No single cause dominates. Death in Nigeria is young, varied, and — crucially — often preventable.

This isn't just data. It is the difference between a grandmother dying at 78 from heart failure and a toddler dying at 2 from diarrhea. Both are tragedies. But one represents the limits of modern medicine; the other represents the failure to deliver basic care that already exists.

Chapter Two

Shifts in Mortality Patterns Across Countries

Mortality data rarely changes through a single dramatic break. It shifts through accumulation: more people, older populations, longer exposure to chronic risk, and uneven progress in prevention.

A Bigger World Produces More Deaths

Global death counts rise steadily across the period, anchored by the countries with the largest populations. India and China remain the dominant contributors, but the shapes of their trajectories differ: India grows more gradually, while China steepens more visibly later in the series.

Rising deaths do not automatically imply worsening health conditions. Part of the increase reflects a world with more people and more older people, which means the global count can grow even as some specific causes become more preventable.

The indexed trends deepen the story. Several countries classified as improvers make rapid early gains and then flatten out around the mid-2000s. Others, especially South Africa and Lesotho, surge in the wrong direction before easing back. Nigeria is less dramatic, but it also never settles into a clear downward path.

That shape matters. It suggests that preventable mortality behaves like a systems indicator: progress can be accelerated, interrupted, or partially reversed.

Prevention works, but it works unevenly — and it does not keep working automatically.

The World Is Living Long Enough to Die Differently

Non-communicable diseases now occupy the center of gravity. Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause and widens its lead over time. Cancers and chronic respiratory disease climb steadily, reinforcing that longer-lived populations increasingly die from chronic, accumulated risk rather than acute infection.

At the same time, tuberculosis, diarrheal disease, and neonatal disorders fall, while HIV/AIDS follows a different arc: it rises sharply early, peaks, and then recedes with the expansion of treatment and prevention.

An Unfinished Transition

Total mortality continues to rise because there are more people, more older people, and more years lived under chronic risk. At the same time, many of the most preventable deaths are falling, especially those tied to early-life conditions and infectious disease.

That duality is the report's central insight. Global mortality is not one problem with one solution. It is a layered story that asks countries to do two things at once: finish the unfinished work of prevention, and build health systems strong enough for the long wave of cardiovascular disease, cancer, and chronic respiratory illness.

"The way we die is changing. The question is whether we are changing fast enough to keep up."

Chapter Three

How Income Shapes the Dominant Path of Death

The shift toward chronic disease does not happen evenly. It depends on income and unfolds at different speeds.

High-income countries remain far ahead, and the gap persists over time. In wealthy nations, cardiovascular diseases dominate every decade, though their share declines from about 42% in 1990 to around 33% in 2019. At the same time, neoplasms rise steadily. The burden shifts within chronic disease, rather than disappearing.

Low-income countries present a starkly different picture. Cardiovascular diseases increase over time but remain less dominant. Neoplasms stay relatively low. Other causes — infectious, neonatal, nutritional — continue to take a large share.

Low-income countries are not replacing one set of diseases with another. They are facing both at once. The gap between high- and low-income groups shows that the transition is not uniform.

The next era of mortality policy will be defined by whether the world can reduce avoidable loss without losing ground to chronic disease. The next chapter of this story will be written by how the world responds — with prevention, policy, and the political will to fight the slow killers as fiercely as it once fought the fast ones.
